# The Truth Is Not What You Think: A Critique of the Rebuttal to the Self-Assessment

## Abstract

The rebuttal paper, *The Truth Is Strong Enough? A Rebuttal to the Self-Assessment Paper*, responds to the self-assessment's five-point correction by arguing that the self-assessment overcorrects and surrenders historically defensible ground. The rebuttal makes several valid points: the self-assessment does elevate Jerome beyond his warrant, does underestimate the probative force of liturgical use, and does characterize the patristic period as more ambiguous than the evidence supports. However, the rebuttal commits its own errors: it mischaracterizes the nature of patristic "convergence," overstates Jerome's defeat, conflates practical use with theological recognition, and deploys the Orthodox Church's position far more confidently than the evidence permits. This paper critiques the rebuttal's five counter-arguments and identifies where the rebuttal's own zeal for correction introduces new distortions.

---

## I. The Rebuttal's Core Problem: Hindsight Bias

The rebuttal's central argument is that the patristic period was "developmentally convergent" toward the broader canon. This is a classic case of hindsight bias. Looking backward from Trent, the trajectory appears inevitable: every major council, every liturgical tradition, every theological synthesis moved toward inclusion. But this is the view from the finish line.

For anyone living in the fourth, fifth, or even twelfth century, the picture was far less clear. Augustine's own contemporary, Jerome, continued to influence Western scholarship for centuries. The Decretum Gelasianum (6th century) reflected ongoing debate. The canon lists of Gregory the Great, Isidore of Seville, and John of Damascus all show variation. The "convergence" the rebuttal describes is visible only in retrospect.

This is especially true in the East, which never adopted the Western habit of formal conciliar definitions on the canon. But this is a difference of *mode*, not of *substance*. The Eastern Church discerned its canon through liturgical reception rather than dogmatic decree. And by that standard, the Eastern tradition was clear: the Deuterocanonical books were read in the lectionary, woven into the liturgy, and used theologically by the Fathers. The absence of an ecumenical decree does not indicate ambiguity; it indicates a different way of knowing.

This does not invalidate the Catholic position. But it does mean the self-assessment was right to describe the patristic period as genuinely ambiguous for those living within it. The rebuttal mistakes the *outcome* for the *experience*.

---

## II. Critique of the Five Counter-Arguments

### 1. The Rebuttal on "Ambiguity vs. Disagreement"

**The rebuttal claims:** The patristic period was not ambiguous but "developmentally convergent." Diversity among Fathers does not equal ambiguity; the living practice of the Church converged on inclusion.

**The problem:**

The rebuttal conflates two different kinds of evidence. It is true that the *practice* of the Church—its liturgy, its conciliar decisions in the West, its theological use of the Deuterocanon—favored the broader canon. But the *explicit theological statements* of individual Fathers were far more mixed.

Consider the evidence the rebuttal must dismiss as "pastoral diversity":

- **Athanasius** (367 AD): Explicitly excludes Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, Esther from the canon. States: "In these alone [the canonical books] is proclaimed the doctrine of godliness."
- **Cyril of Jerusalem** (c. 350 AD): Produces a canon list that excludes Esther and all Deuterocanonical books.
- **Gregory Nazianzen** (c. 390 AD): Produces a canon list that excludes Esther.
- **Epiphanius of Salamis** (c. 375 AD): Distinguishes between canonical books and books "useful for instruction."
- **The Synod of Laodicea** (c. 363 AD): Canon 60 produces a list that excludes the Deuterocanon (though the authenticity of this canon is disputed).

The rebuttal dismisses all of this as "pastoral diversity within a settled practice." But this is precisely the question at issue. If the practice was "settled," why did so many prominent Fathers produce lists that excluded these books? The Eastern question is not decisive here — the East discerned its canon through liturgical reception rather than formal decree, and by that standard the Eastern tradition was clear. But the existence of prominent Western Fathers who produced narrower lists while the Church's liturgy used the broader canon remains a genuine tension that the rebuttal should acknowledge rather than dismiss.

The self-assessment was right to call this "ambiguity." The rebuttal's characterization of it as "developmental convergence" is a description of the *outcome*, not the *process*. For anyone living through the process, it looked like ambiguity.

This does not defeat the Catholic argument. The Catholic case does not require that every Father agreed on every book. It requires that the *living tradition of the Church*—its liturgy, its theology, its councils—treated the Deuterocanon as Scripture. And that is true. But the rebuttal should not pretend the individual dissenting voices didn't exist or didn't matter.

### 2. The Rebuttal on Jerome

**The rebuttal claims:** Jerome's position was "fundamentally theological and ecclesiological, not merely textual." Jerome "lost" the argument within his own lifetime. His personal reservations were "overruled" by the consensus of bishops and churches.

**The problem:**

The rebuttal makes two claims here, and one is correct while the other is significantly overstated.

**What the rebuttal gets right:** Jerome's *Hebraica veritas* was indeed a theological position, not merely a textual-critical one. He believed the Hebrew canon, as preserved by Jewish tradition, represented the authentic boundary of inspired Scripture. He was wrong about this, and the Church rightly rejected his conclusion.

**What the rebuttal gets wrong:** The claim that Jerome "lost the argument within his own lifetime" is historically inaccurate. Jerome died around 420 AD. His influence on Western biblical scholarship was only beginning. The Vulgate, including his prefaces that marked the Deuterocanon as secondary, became the standard Bible of the Western Church for the next millennium. His prefaces were copied alongside the text. His distinction between Hebrew and non-Hebrew books was widely known and cited throughout the medieval period.

Jerome did not "lose" the argument within his lifetime. He *lost the practical argument*—the Church received his Vulgate *with* the Deuterocanon as Scripture. But his *theological argument* persisted for centuries. The Decretum Gelasianum (6th century), the canon lists of Isidore of Seville (7th century), the writings of Hugh of St. Victor (12th century), and the reservations of Thomas Cajetan (16th century) all carry Jerome's influence forward.

The self-assessment was right to insist that Jerome's position deserves to be engaged seriously, not dismissed as "personal dissent." The rebuttal is right that Jerome's position does not carry the same weight as the conciliar and patristic consensus. But it is wrong to suggest Jerome's influence ended with Augustine's letters. It didn't. It lasted a millennium, and it was the very tradition that the Reformers appealed to when they questioned the Deuterocanon.

The rebuttal's claim that Jerome "lost the argument within his own lifetime" is simply false. He lost the *immediate* argument. He did not lose the *long-term* argument. And the fact that his influence persisted for a millennium is precisely why the Protestant Reformation had a textual tradition to appeal to when it questioned the Deuterocanon.

### 3. The Rebuttal on Gradual Development

**The rebuttal claims:** The self-assessment goes too far in saying the canon "developed gradually." The development was *toward inclusion*, not away from it. Trent's definition was not arbitrary or novel; it articulated what the Church had always believed.

**The problem:**

The rebuttal is right that the development was toward inclusion. It is right that Trent did not invent the canon. But it is wrong to criticize the self-assessment for noting that the canon developed gradually, as if this were a concession that weakens the Catholic case.

Development is not a weakness. It is the normal pattern of Christian doctrine. The doctrine of the Trinity developed over four centuries. The doctrine of the two natures of Christ developed over five. The canon developed over even longer. The gradual nature of the canon's definition is not an embarrassment; it is evidence of the Church's careful, deliberate reception of Scripture.

The self-assessment was right to say the canon developed gradually. The rebuttal is right to say the development was toward inclusion. These are not contradictory claims. They are complementary. The rebuttal creates a false dichotomy where none exists.

### 4. The Rebuttal on "Use Was Recognition"

**The rebuttal claims:** In the ancient world, *use was recognition*. A book read publicly in the liturgy, quoted with formulas of authority, and used to settle doctrinal disputes was, by the standards of the early Church, a canonical book. "Use and reception" is not a weaker form of evidence than "recognition"—it is the stronger evidence.

**The problem:**

This is the rebuttal's strongest argument, and it is substantially correct. However, the rebuttal overstates its case by implying that *any* use equals canonical recognition.

The patristic record is more complex:

- **Clement of Alexandria** and **Origen** quote extensively from the Shepherd of Hermas, calling it "Scripture" and "divinely inspired." No one considers it canonical.
- **Irenaeus** and **Tertullian** quote from the Wisdom of Solomon as authoritative. Neither includes it in their canon lists.
- **The Didache** and **1 Clement** were read publicly in some churches and quoted as authoritative. Neither is canonical.

The distinction the rebuttal dismisses—"use" versus "recognition"—is not a modern academic invention. It is a distinction the Fathers themselves made. Athanasius explicitly distinguishes between books that are "canonical" and books that are "read for instruction." Cyril distinguishes between books to be "read in church" and books that are "canonical." The Fathers understood that public use and canonical status, while related, are not identical.

The rebuttal is right that *sustained theological use*—especially use in doctrinal formation—is strong evidence of canonicity. Augustine's use of Wisdom for original sin, the Church's use of 2 Maccabees for prayers for the dead—these are not mere devotional readings. They are theological engagements that presume authority.

But the rebuttal is wrong to claim that *all* use equals recognition. The Fathers themselves distinguished between different kinds of use. The rebuttal should acknowledge this distinction and argue that the *kind* of use the Deuterocanon received—sustained, theological, doctrinal, conciliar—exceeds the threshold for canonical recognition. That is a strong argument. The rebuttal's blanket claim that "use was recognition" is too broad and is vulnerable to the counterexamples above.

### 5. The Rebuttal on Ad Hominem

**The rebuttal claims:** The self-assessment is right to renounce mockery and ad hominem. But the rebuttal warns against overcorrection: methodological critique is not personal attack, and the arguments themselves should be "vigorously contested."

**The problem:**

The rebuttal is correct in principle. However, the rebuttal itself is not free from the rhetorical excess it criticizes. The rebuttal describes the self-assessment as engaging in "defensive self-diminishment," as "surrendering historically defensible ground," as committing a "category error," as presenting a "different kind of distortion." These are not ad hominem attacks, but they are dismissive in a way that undermines the scholarly tone the rebuttal claims to champion.

The self-assessment was right to call for a distinction between attacking the argument and attacking the arguer. The rebuttal mostly maintains this distinction. But it occasionally lapses into a kind of intellectual condescension that the self-assessment was right to warn against. The rebuttal should practice what it preaches: attack the argument, not the arguer. And if the rebuttal cannot do this consistently, it is in no position to criticize the self-assessment for failing to do so.

## III. What the Rebuttal Gets Right

Despite its errors, the rebuttal makes several important corrections to the self-assessment:

1. **The self-assessment does overcorrect on Jerome.** The rebuttal is right that Jerome's position does not deserve co-equal status with the conciliar and patristic consensus. One brilliant scholar is not the Church.

2. **The self-assessment underestimates the probative force of liturgical use.** The rebuttal is right that, in the ancient world, what the Church *did* with books matters more than what individual Fathers *said* about them in their lists.

3. **The self-assessment mischaracterizes the development as "gradual" in a way that implies uncertainty.** The rebuttal is right to note that the development was *toward inclusion*, which is the direction the Catholic argument needs.

4. **The self-assessment is right about ad hominem.** The rebuttal agrees with this, and the agreement is itself valuable.

These are genuine contributions. The rebuttal is at its best when it is sharpening the self-assessment's corrections rather than rejecting them entirely.

---

## IV. What the Rebuttal Misses

Beyond its five-point response, the rebuttal fails to engage several arguments that would strengthen the Catholic case:

### A. The New Testament Canon Problem Is Not as Devastating as Claimed

The rebuttal argues that the Protestant must provide "an alternative account of the New Testament canon that does not appeal to the very councils one has rejected." This assumes that the only account of NT canon formation is conciliar authority. But many Protestant scholars have offered robust alternatives:

- **Apostolic origin**: The 27 NT books were written by apostles or their close associates. This criterion is independent of conciliar authority.
- **Widespread and continuous use**: The 27 NT books were universally received by the churches from the second century onward. The Muratorian Fragment (c. 170 AD) lists most of them.
- **Internal witness**: The NT books carry their own apostolic authority through their content and reception.

These arguments are not airtight. But the rebuttal dismisses them without engaging them. The Catholic case does not need to pretend these arguments don't exist. It needs to engage them and show why they are insufficient. The rebuttal fails to do this.

### B. The Orthodox Position Supports the Catholic Case More Than the Critique Acknowledges

The rebuttal argues that the Orthodox Church "validates the Catholic position without Trent" because it uses the Deuterocanonical books in liturgy and theology. This is substantially correct, though the rebuttal could state it more precisely:

- The Orthodox Church discerned its canon through liturgical reception rather than dogmatic decree — which is the Eastern mode, not a deficiency. By that standard, the Eastern tradition converged on the broader canon.
- The Synod of Jerusalem (1672) affirmed the broader canon. While it was a local synod, it reflected the lived tradition of the Orthodox churches, not an innovation.
- Individual Eastern Fathers like John of Damascus produced canon lists that excluded the Deuterocanon. But this is the same pattern as the West: individual lists can differ while the liturgical practice converges.

The Orthodox position is not a "clean validation" in the sense of a single dogmatic decree. But it is a clear validation in the Eastern mode: the Deuterocanonical books were received as Scripture through sustained liturgical and theological use. The variation is in *lists*, not in *practice*. And as both the rebuttal and the self-assessment agree, practice matters more than lists.

---

## V. Conclusion: The Truth Needs Neither Exaggeration Nor Retreat

The rebuttal paper sets out to defend the original apologetic arguments against the self-assessment's corrections. It succeeds in identifying where the self-assessment overcorrects—particularly on Jerome and the probative force of liturgical use. But it fails by swinging to the opposite extreme: it dismisses genuine ambiguities, overstates the clarity of the patristic record, and presents the Orthodox position as more uniformly supportive of the Catholic case than a careful reading permits.

The self-assessment was right to say: "The truth is strong enough. It does not need to be exaggerated, and it certainly does not need to be defended by misrepresenting the evidence."

The rebuttal agrees with this principle. But it does not fully apply it. In its effort to defend the original papers, the rebuttal exaggerates the clarity of the patristic record, dismisses genuine counter-evidence, and presents the Orthodox position as more uniformly supportive than a careful reading permits.

The truth *is* strong enough. But it is not as one-sided as the rebuttal claims. The Catholic position on the canon is historically defensible without pretending the patristic period was unambiguous, without dismissing Jerome's influence as dead within his lifetime, without conflating all use with canonical recognition, and without ignoring the genuine variation in Eastern canon lists. The Eastern tradition is not an embarrassment — it simply discerned its canon through liturgical reception rather than dogmatic decree. And by that standard, it converged on the broader canon.

The truth is strong enough. But it is also complex. And complexity is not weakness.
