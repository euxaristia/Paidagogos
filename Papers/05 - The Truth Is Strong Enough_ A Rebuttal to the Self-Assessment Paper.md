# The Truth Is Strong Enough? A Rebuttal to the Self-Assessment Paper

## Abstract

The self-assessment paper, *A Critical Self-Assessment: Accuracies, Inaccuracies, and the Canonical Question*, undertakes a valuable exercise in intellectual honesty. It identifies genuine strengths in the original apologetic arguments—the Jamnia myth, the Septuagint's primacy, the pre-Tridentine conciliar consensus—and renounces real weaknesses, including rhetorical excess and overstated claims about Athanasius and Jerome. However, in its zeal for self-correction, the self-assessment paper overcorrects. It introduces new errors, abandons defensible positions too readily, and in some cases retreats from claims that are historically warranted. This paper offers a rebuttal to the self-assessment's five-point "more honest position," arguing that the Catholic case is stronger than the self-assessment allows, that the proposed "corrections" create more problems than they solve, and that a genuinely honest debate requires neither exaggeration nor unnecessary concession.

---

## I. The Danger of False Equivalence

The self-assessment paper's central methodological impulse is to appear balanced by conceding points to the opposing side. This is admirable in principle but dangerous in execution. The paper criticizes the original papers for overstating their case, then proceeds to understate it. The result is not balance but a different kind of distortion: a defensive posture that surrenders historically defensible ground in the name of scholarly humility.

The self-assessment's conclusion proposes five corrections. This paper addresses each in turn.

---

## II. Rebuttal: The Five "Corrections" Examined

### 1. "Acknowledge the genuine ambiguities in the patristic period"

**The self-assessment claims:** The patristic period was genuinely ambiguous about the canon, and the original papers should have acknowledged this rather than presenting a unified front.

**Rebuttal:**

The self-assessment confuses *ambiguity* with *disagreement*. It is true that individual Fathers expressed different views on individual books. Athanasius, Cyril of Jerusalem, and Gregory Nazianzen produced lists that excluded some or all of the Deuterocanonical books. But what the self-assessment calls "ambiguity" is better described as *pastoral diversity within a settled practice*.

The key question is not whether every Father agreed on every book, but whether the *living practice of the Church*—its liturgy, its theology, its conciliar decisions—treated the Deuterocanonical books as Scripture. On that question, the evidence is not ambiguous:

- The Councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397) produced a definitive list that included the Deuterocanon.
- Augustine, the dominant theological voice of the Western Church for a millennium, treated these books as canonical and built foundational doctrines upon them.
- The Vulgate, Jerome's own translation, included the Deuterocanon and was received as the Church's Bible.
- The liturgical use of these books in both East and West was continuous and unbroken.

This is not "ambiguity." This is a living tradition with internal diversity that nevertheless converged on a consistent practice. The self-assessment is right to note that the Deuterocanon was not *universally* recognized as canonical from the start. But "universally from the start" is an impossible standard for any element of early Christian tradition. The New Testament canon itself was disputed for centuries—Hebrews, James, 2 Peter, Revelation, and others faced significant opposition. If we apply the self-assessment's standard of "ambiguity" consistently, we would have to declare the entire biblical canon uncertain.

The self-assessment commits a category error: it mistakes the *process* of reception for the *absence* of consensus. The patristic period was not ambiguous; it was developmental, and the development clearly favored inclusion.

### 2. "Engage Jerome's textual arguments on their merits"

**The self-assessment claims:** Jerome was "the premier biblical scholar of the ancient Church" and his *Hebraica veritas* position is a "legitimate scholarly concern" that the original papers should have engaged "seriously" rather than dismissing as "personal dissent."

**Rebuttal:**

This is the self-assessment's most significant overcorrection. Jerome's position deserves to be *stated accurately*—which the self-assessment itself fails to do—but it does not deserve the deference the self-assessment grants it.

First, the self-assessment misrepresents what Jerome actually argued. Jerome did not simply "prefer the Hebrew text" as a modern textual critic might. His argument was fundamentally *theological and ecclesiological*, not merely textual:

- Jerome believed that the Hebrew canon, as preserved by Jewish tradition, represented the authentic boundary of inspired Scripture.
- He argued that books not found in the Hebrew should be read for edification (*ad aedificationem plebis*) but not for confirming doctrine (*ad auctoritatem ecclesiae de dogmatibus confirmandam*).
- He translated the Deuterocanonical books under compulsion from church authorities, not conviction, and marked them as secondary in his own prefaces.

These are not "textual arguments" in the modern sense. They are theological arguments about the relationship between Jewish and Christian Scripture. And they are arguments that Jerome *lost* within his own lifetime.

Augustine directly responded to Jerome in *Letter 28* and *Letter 82*, and Jerome conceded the point in practice. The Church received Jerome's Vulgate *with* the Deuterocanon as Scripture. His personal reservations were overruled not by "institutional authority" suppressing a brilliant scholar, but by the consensus of the bishops and the practice of the churches he served.

More importantly, Jerome's *Hebraica veritas* principle has a fatal historical problem: **the Hebrew text Jerome used was not the text of the Apostles.** The Masoretic Text tradition that Jerome consulted was a post-Christian development. The Dead Sea Scrolls, discovered centuries after Jerome, proved that the textual landscape of first-century Judaism was far more pluralistic than Jerome knew. The "Hebrew canon" Jerome appealed to was not a fixed, first-century reality. It was a developing tradition.

To treat Jerome's position as a "legitimate scholarly concern" on par with the Augustinian consensus is to privilege the lone scholar over the living Church. Jerome was brilliant, but he was one voice. The councils of Hippo and Carthage, the liturgical practice of East and West, the theological use of the Deuterocanon by Augustine, Ambrose, Gregory of Nyssa, and the rest of the patristic corpus—these represent the mind of the Church. Jerome represents the mind of Jerome.

The self-assessment is right to insist that Jerome's position be *stated accurately*. It is wrong to elevate it to a position of co-equal authority with the conciliar and patristic consensus.

### 3. "Recognize that the canon developed gradually, not through a single conciliar act"

**The self-assessment claims:** The original papers wrongly claimed that Hippo and Carthage "settled" the canon. The canon developed gradually, and Trent was the first dogmatic definition.

**Rebuttal:**

This is the self-assessment's most accurate correction. The original papers *did* overstate when they claimed Hippo and Carthage "settled" the canon for the *entire* Church. These were regional North African councils, and their authority was not ecumenical.

However, the self-assessment goes too far in the other direction. It is not enough to say the canon "developed gradually." The question is: *in what direction did it develop?*

The development was *toward inclusion*, not away from it. Every major conciliar decision, every liturgical tradition, every major theological synthesis in the first fifteen centuries of the Church moved *toward* the broader canon. There is no counter-movement of comparable weight. Even Jerome, who argued for the narrower canon, translated the broader one. Even the Eastern Fathers who produced narrower lists continued to *use* the Deuterocanonical books theologically.

The self-assessment is right that Trent was the first *dogmatic, ecumenical* definition of the canon. But it is wrong to imply that this makes Trent's definition arbitrary or novel. Dogmatic definitions do not create truth; they articulate what the Church has always believed. The Nicene Creed did not "invent" the divinity of Christ. The Chalcedonian Definition did not "invent" the two natures. Trent did not "invent" the canon. It defined it.

The self-assessment should have said: Hippo and Carthage did not *settle* the canon in an ecumenical sense, but they *codified* the Western Church's practice. Trent *dogmatized* this codified tradition in response to a new challenge. The development was real, but it was development *of* apostolic tradition, not departure *from* it.

### 4. "Focus on the *use* and *reception* of the Deuterocanonical books rather than claiming they were universally recognized as canonical from the start"

**The self-assessment claims:** The original papers should stop claiming universal early recognition and instead focus on how the Deuterocanonical books were *used* and *received* by the Church.

**Rebuttal:**

This correction is largely sound, but the self-assessment underestimates the probative force of *use and reception* itself.

In the ancient world, *use was recognition*. A book read publicly in the liturgy, quoted in theological treatises with formulas of authority ("as it is written," "Scripture says"), and used to settle doctrinal disputes was, by the standards of the early Church, a canonical book. The early Church did not have a modern academic concept of "canonical status" that could be separated from liturgical and theological use. The two were inseparable.

When Augustine quotes Wisdom of Solomon to define original sin, he is not engaging in "moral exhortation." He is doing dogmatic theology. When the author of Hebrews builds an argument for the resurrection on the Maccabean martyrs, he is not collecting "interesting stories." He is constructing a theological argument on a scriptural foundation. When 2 Maccabees 12:43-46 becomes the basis for the Church's practice of praying for the dead, the book is functioning as Scripture.

The self-assessment is right to shift the argument from "universal recognition from the start" to "use and reception." But it should recognize that *use and reception, in the ancient context, is recognition*. The Deuterocanonical books were not "universally recognized" in the abstract, academic sense. They were recognized in the only way that mattered: the Church used them as Scripture.

The self-assessment's correction here is directionally correct but undercuts its own point by treating "use" as a weaker form of evidence than "recognition." In the patristic world, it is the stronger evidence. Anyone can write a list. Lists are cheap. What matters is what the Church *did* with the books it read week after week, century after century.

### 5. "Avoid ad hominem rhetoric and treat opponents with the intellectual respect they deserve"

**The self-assessment claims:** The original papers should abandon mockery, ad hominem attacks, and appeals to the supposed intellectual bankruptcy of opponents.

**Rebuttal:**

This correction is entirely correct. The self-assessment is right to renounce the rhetorical excesses of the original papers. The use of Matthew 7:6 ("Do not give dogs what is holy") against interlocutors, the celebration of "the opponent's laughter," the suggestion of "surgical irony"—these are not scholarly argument. They are the intellectual equivalent of punching down.

However, the self-assessment should not swing to the opposite extreme and imply that *all* polemic is inappropriate. There is a difference between ad hominem attack and honest critique of method. The original papers were right to criticize the *method* of Protestant apologetics—the selective reading of patristic sources, the appeal to the debunked Jamnia myth, the logical contradiction of accepting Carthage's canon while rejecting Carthage's authority. These are methodological critiques, not personal attacks. They should be sharpened, not abandoned.

The distinction is this: attack the argument, not the arguer. The original papers sometimes failed to maintain this distinction. The self-assessment is right to call it out. But it should not go further and imply that the arguments themselves should not be vigorously contested.

## III. What the Self-Assessment Misses Entirely

Beyond its five-point correction, the self-assessment paper misses several arguments that the original papers should have pressed harder:

### A. The Dead Sea Scrolls Undermine the Entire Hebrew Canon Argument

The self-assessment mentions the Dead Sea Scrolls only briefly. But their discovery is devastating to the Protestant appeal to a fixed Hebrew canon. The Qumran community possessed and valuedied texts of the Deuterocanonical books alongside protocanonical ones. The textual pluralism of first-century Judaism means there was no "Hebrew canon" for the Apostles to follow or reject. The canon was fluid. The Church's choice of the Septuagint was not a deviation from a settled standard; it was a choice among living traditions.

### B. The Orthodox Church Validates the Catholic Position Without Trent

The self-assessment correctly notes that the Eastern Orthodox Church has never formally defined a closed canon. But it fails to draw the obvious conclusion: the Orthodox Church *uses* the Deuterocanonical books in its liturgy, its lectionaries, and its theological tradition. The Orthodox position demonstrates that one can affirm the Deuterocanonical books as Scripture without appealing to Trent. The canon is not a Western invention; it is the inheritance of the undivided Church.

### C. The New Testament Canon Has the Same Problem

The self-assessment does not address the most devastating question for the Protestant position: by what authority does one accept the 27-book New Testament canon? The same councils (Hippo, Carthage) that affirmed the Deuterocanon also affirmed the New Testament canon. If one rejects the authority of these councils to define the Old Testament canon, one must provide an alternative account of the New Testament canon that does not appeal to the very councils one has rejected. This is the argument the self-assessment should have pressed, not retreated from.

---

## IV. Conclusion: The Truth Does Not Need Rescue

The self-assessment paper concludes: "The Catholic position on the canon is historically defensible. It does not need to be exaggerated, and it certainly does not need to be defended by misrepresenting the evidence. The truth is strong enough."

This is exactly right. But the self-assessment does not fully apply its own principle. In its effort to avoid exaggeration, it retreats from claims that are historically defensible. It elevates Jerome to co-equal status with the conciliar consensus. It treats "use and reception" as weaker evidence than it actually was in the ancient context. It characterizes the patristic period as "ambiguous" when it was, in fact, developmentally convergent.

The truth *is* strong enough. The Catholic position on the canon does not need exaggeration, but it also does not need the kind of defensive self-diminishment the self-assessment proposes. The Septuagint was the Bible of the Apostles. The Deuterocanonical books were used theologically by the Fathers. The councils of Hippo and Carthage codified a living tradition. Trent dogmatized it. The Orthodox Church affirms it without Trent. The Dead Sea Scrolls demolish the Hebrew canon argument. The New Testament canon stands or falls by the same authority as the Old.

None of this requires exaggeration. None of it requires misrepresentation. None of it requires mockery or ad hominem. But none of it requires retreat either.

The truth is strong enough. Let it stand.
