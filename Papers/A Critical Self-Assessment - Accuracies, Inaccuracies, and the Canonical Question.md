# A Critical Self-Assessment: Accuracies, Inaccuracies, and the Canonical Question

## Abstract

This paper provides a critical meta-analysis of three previously published apologetic works defending the Catholic biblical canon against Protestant objections: *The Unbroken Chain: Refuting the Two-Tiered Canon Thesis from Patristic Lists to Apostolic Doctrine*, *From Edification to Doctrine: The Unbroken Witness of the Early Church Against a Two-Tiered Canon*, and *The Council of Trent Did Not Create the Canon: Refuting Protestant Objections to Deuterocanonical Authority*. While these papers collectively advance sound historical and theological arguments regarding the development of the biblical canon—including the refutation of the Jamnia myth, the doctrinal use of Deuterocanonical texts by New Testament authors, and the pre-Tridentine conciliar consensus at Hippo and Carthage—they also exhibit significant methodological weaknesses, overstated claims, and historical inaccuracies that undermine their credibility. This paper identifies and restates the papers' enduring contributions while honestly renouncing their errors, excesses, and polemical distortions. The goal is intellectual honesty: to preserve what is true, correct what is false, and model the scholarly integrity the debate demands.

---

## Introduction

The question of the biblical canon—its contents, its development, and its authority—is among the most significant dividing lines between Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant traditions. The three papers analyzed here were written as polemical defenses of the Catholic position, responding to Protestant claims that (a) the Council of Trent created the canon in 1546, (b) the Deuterocanonical books lack doctrinal authority, and (c) the early Church Fathers maintained a two-tiered view of Scripture distinguishing inspired books from merely useful ones.

These papers succeed in identifying several genuine weaknesses in common Protestant apologetic arguments. However, they also repeat errors, overstate their case, engage in the very proof-texting they criticize in opponents, and occasionally substitute rhetoric for argument. This paper offers a balanced assessment: affirming what is historically and theologically sound, renouncing what is not, and suggesting a more rigorous path forward.

---

## I. Accuracies: What the Papers Get Right

### A. The "Council of Jamnia" Is a Myth

All three papers correctly identify that the widely repeated claim of a "Council of Jamnia" (c. 90 AD) that definitively closed the Hebrew canon is not supported by modern scholarship. The papers accurately cite the consensus among historians—including Shaye J.D. Cohen, Jack P. Lewis, and Albert C. Sundberg—that the evidence points not to a formal council but to ongoing rabbinic discussions at Yavneh (Jamnia) after the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 AD.

**What is accurate:**
- No single event or council at Jamnia closed the Hebrew canon.
- The canonization process was gradual, spanning centuries, with debates about certain books (Ezekiel, Proverbs, Song of Songs, Esther) continuing well into the rabbinic period.
- Even if discussions occurred at Jamnia, they post-date the New Testament writings and cannot serve as the standard for what the Apostles considered Scripture.
- The Masoretic Text as a fixed, universally recognized canon did not exist in the first century AD.

This is the papers' strongest and most defensible contribution. Any argument that appeals to Jamnia as the arbiter of the Old Testament canon rests on discredited historiography and should be abandoned by all sides.

### B. The Septuagint Was the Bible of the Early Church

The papers correctly emphasize that the Septuagint (LXX)—the Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures—was the primary scriptural text used by the New Testament authors and the early Church. This is well-documented and uncontroversial among scholars.

**What is accurate:**
- The New Testament quotes the Old Testament far more often from the Septuagint than from any Hebrew textual tradition.
- The Septuagint manuscripts available in the first century included the Deuterocanonical books (or at least the material that would become them).
- The early Church Fathers, both Eastern and Western, used the Septuagint and its expanded corpus extensively.
- Privileging a later, narrower Hebrew canon over the Septuagint requires explaining why the Apostles' Bible should be replaced by a canon solidified after the Church's founding.

This is a strong historical argument. The Septuagint's inclusion of the Deuterocanonical material, combined with its use by the New Testament writers, provides genuine grounds for questioning a hard-and-fast boundary that excludes these books.

### C. The Councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397) Predate Trent

The papers correctly document that the canon including the Deuterocanonical books was affirmed at the Councils of Hippo (393 AD) and Carthage (397 AD)—over a millennium before Trent. This is historically accurate and directly refutes the claim that Trent "invented" the Catholic canon.

**What is accurate:**
- These North African councils listed a canon of 46 Old Testament books (including the Deuterocanon) and 27 New Testament books.
- Augustine was present at these councils and strongly advocated for the broader canon.
- The Western Church largely accepted this canon for the next thousand years.
- Trent's 1546 decree was a *reaffirmation* of this existing tradition in response to Reformation challenges, not a novel creation.

The claim that Trent "created" the canon is historically false and easily disproven. The papers are right to press this point.

### D. New Testament Use of Deuterocanonical Material

The papers correctly identify several instances where New Testament authors appear to draw upon Deuterocanonical texts:

**What is accurate:**
- Hebrews 11:35 references martyrs who "were tortured, refusing to accept release, so that they might rise again to a better life"—a clear parallel to 2 Maccabees 6–7 (the seven brothers and their mother). No passage in the protocanonical books describes this event.
- James 2:23 calls Abraham "the friend of God," a phrase that appears in Sirach 44:20 (in connection with Abraham) but not in the Genesis account.
- The practice of praying for the dead (2 Maccabees 12:43–46) has early Christian parallels, and the Maccabean text provides the most direct Old Testament precedent.
- Augustine explicitly used Wisdom of Solomon to articulate the doctrine of original sin and Sirach in debates against Pelagianism.

These are legitimate examples of Deuterocanonical influence on early Christian theology. They do not *prove* the canonicity of these books, but they do demonstrate that the authors of the New Testament and the early Fathers found them theologically useful and authoritative.

### E. The Critique of "Selective Magisterialism"

The papers raise a valid logical challenge: if one accepts the canon list from Carthage (397 AD) as authoritative, on what basis does one reject the *authority* of the council that produced it? This is a genuine tension in Protestant apologetics that deserves a more careful response than it typically receives.

**What is accurate:**
- Many Protestants accept the 27-book New Testament canon largely because the same councils (Hippo, Carthage) that affirmed the Deuterocanon also affirmed it.
- If the councils had the authority to settle the New Testament canon, why not the Old Testament canon?
- If they lacked the authority, then the Protestant acceptance of the New Testament canon requires an alternative justification (which is possible but must be argued, not assumed).

This is a strong internal critique that forces Protestant apologists to confront an inconsistency in their position.

## II. Inaccuracies: What the Papers Get Wrong

### A. Overstatement: Athanasius's Distinction Was "Merely Pastoral"

**The claim:** All three papers argue that Athanasius's distinction between canonical and "read" books in his 39th Festal Letter was one of "pastoral purpose," "usage and familiarity," or "pedagogical reasons"—not of ontological authority or inspiration.

**Why this is overstated or inaccurate:**
Athanasius's own words in the 39th Festal Letter are far stronger than the papers acknowledge. He writes of the canonical books: "In these alone is proclaimed the doctrine of godliness. Let no man add to these, neither let him take ought from these." Of the other books (Wisdom, Sirach, Esther, Judith, Tobit, the Didache, and the Shepherd), he says they are "not indeed included in the Canon, but are appointed by the Fathers to be read by those who newly join us and who wish for instruction in the word of godliness."

The phrase "not indeed included in the Canon" (*ouk onta en tō kanoni*) is unambiguous. Athanasius is making an ontological claim, not merely a pastoral one. He is saying these books are *not* in the canon. To claim otherwise is eisegesis. The papers are simply wrong on this point.

**What should be renounced:** The claim that Athanasius did not genuinely distinguish between canonical and non-canonical books. He did. The papers should acknowledge this honestly and then argue that Athanasius's view does not represent the consensus of the early Church—which is a much stronger and more defensible position.

### B. Misrepresentation of Jerome's Position

**The claim:** The papers repeatedly characterize Jerome's reservations about the Deuterocanon as "personal dissent" that was "subsumed by the authority of the institution he served."

**Why this is inaccurate:**
Jerome was not merely expressing a "personal preference." He was the foremost biblical scholar of the early Church, and his position was far more nuanced than the papers suggest:

1. Jerome explicitly distinguished between the *Hebraica veritas* (the Hebrew truth) and the Greek/Latin additions.
2. In his *Prologus Galeatus* (Helmeted Preface), Jerome clearly stated that books outside the Hebrew canon should be read for edification but not for establishing doctrine.
3. Jerome did translate Tobit and Judith, but he did so reluctantly, under pressure, and he marked them as non-canonical in his own prefaces.
4. Jerome's position was not marginal; it represented the best textual scholarship of his day, informed by direct consultation of Hebrew manuscripts.

**What should be renounced:** The dismissal of Jerome as a lone scholar whose "personal dissent" was overridden by institutional authority. Jerome was the premier biblical scholar of the ancient Church. His position deserves to be engaged seriously, not caricatured. The papers would be stronger if they acknowledged the weight of Jerome's textual arguments and then responded to them on their merits.

### C. Overstated New Testament Citations

**The claim:** Hebrews 11 cites 2 Maccabees, and James 2:23 cites Sirach, as proof that these books are canonical Scripture.

**Why this is overstated:**
While the parallels are genuine, the papers overstate their probative force:

1. **Hebrews and 2 Maccabees:** The author of Hebrews does not *quote* 2 Maccabees. He alludes to the martyrdom tradition, which may have been known through oral tradition, common Jewish martyrdom literature, or multiple written sources. The parallel is striking, but it is an allusion, not a citation. New Testament authors allude to many non-canonical sources without treating them as Scripture (e.g., Paul's citations of Greek poets in Acts 17:28 and Titus 1:12; Jude's reference to Enoch).

2. **James and Sirach:** The phrase "friend of God" applied to Abraham appears in Sirach 44:20, but it also reflects a broader Jewish tradition about Abraham (cf. Isaiah 41:8; 2 Chronicles 20:7). James may be drawing on this tradition independently of Sirach. Furthermore, James introduces the quote with "the scripture was fulfilled"—referring to Genesis 15:6, which *is* about Abraham's faith being counted as righteousness. The "friend of God" title is an *addition*, not the quoted text.

3. **Jude and Enoch:** The papers cite Jude's quotation of 1 Enoch as evidence that the early Church drew from a "wider pool of sacred literature." But this actually *undermines* the case for the Deuterocanon. Jude quotes from a book that is universally recognized as pseudepigraphal (not even in the Catholic canon). If Jude can quote Enoch without making it canonical, then an allusion to 2 Maccabees does not prove its canonicity either.

**What should be renounced:** The claim that these references *prove* the canonicity of the Deuterocanonical books. They show influence and usage, not canonical status. The papers should be more careful: allusion does not equal canonization.

### D. The Claim That Hippo and Carthage "Settled" the Canon

**The claim:** The papers repeatedly assert that Hippo (393) and Carthage (397) "settled the matter" of the canon for the entire Church, East and West.

**Why this is inaccurate:**
This claim is historically false on multiple levels:

1. **These were regional councils.** Hippo and Carthage were North African synods. They did not claim universal authority, nor were they received as ecumenical by the Eastern Church.
2. **The Eastern Church never accepted their canon.** The Eastern Orthodox Church to this day has not formally defined a closed canon. The Synod of Jerusalem (1672) and earlier lists (e.g., the Synod of Hippo was unknown in the East) show that the Orthodox canon remained somewhat fluid.
3. **Even in the West, debate continued.** Jerome's position was widely known. The Decretum Gelasianum (6th century) reflected ongoing debate. The canon was not "settled" in any absolute sense until much later.
4. **Trent was necessary precisely because the canon had not been dogmatically defined at a universally binding level.** The papers argue that Trent was not needed because Carthage had already "settled" it—but then they cannot explain why Trent felt the need to issue a dogmatic, anathema-enforced definition.

**What should be renounced:** The claim that Hippo and Carthage "settled" the canon. They did not. They reflected a widespread *practice* in the Western Church, but they did not produce an ecumenical, dogmatic definition. Trent was the first council to define the canon with dogmatic, binding force for the entire Catholic Church. The papers should acknowledge this and argue that Trent *confirmed* and *dogmatized* a long-standing tradition, rather than claiming the tradition was already "settled."

### E. Rhetorical Excess and Ad Hominem

**The claim:** The papers occasionally lapse into rhetoric that substitutes mockery for argument. One paper suggests responding to dismissive opponents with "surgical irony," quoting Proverbs 14:9 ("Fools mock at the guilt offering"), and declaring that "the opponent's laughter reveals he has no argument left."

**Why this is problematic:**
This is not scholarship; it is polemical posturing. Several issues arise:

1. **It is a straw man.** Not all opponents of the Deuterocanon are dismissive scoffers. Many Protestant scholars engage the evidence seriously, acknowledge the historical complexities, and offer principled theological reasons for their position (e.g., appeals to the Hebrew canon, textual-critical arguments, or doctrinal concerns).
2. **It is self-defeating.** The papers criticize Protestant "cherry-picking" and "proof-texting" but then engage in the same rhetorical tactics when convenient.
3. **It is uncharitable.** Quoting Matthew 7:6 ("Do not give dogs what is holy") against interlocutors is not "pastoral charity." It is contempt.
4. **It undermines credibility.** A paper that takes itself seriously as scholarly argument should not instruct its readers to mock their opponents.

**What should be renounced:** All ad hominem rhetoric, mockery, and appeals to the supposed intellectual bankruptcy of opponents. The historical and theological arguments stand or fall on their own merits.

### F. Conflation of the Papacy Question with the Canon Question

**The claim:** One paper includes a section on the papacy, arguing that the medieval English Church recognized papal supremacy until 1534, and that "the true locus of apostolic succession, which includes the Petrine office, is preserved within the Catholic Church."

**Why this is problematic:**
This is a separate argument entirely. The question of the biblical canon is not identical to the question of papal authority. The papers should not smuggle a defense of papal primacy into a discussion about the canon, as it conflates two distinct debates and introduces arguments that are irrelevant to the canonical question.

**What should be renounced:** The conflation of the papacy question with the canon question. The papers should stay focused on the canon and avoid importing ecclesiological arguments that belong in a different paper.

## III. A More Honest Position: What Should Be Argued

Having identified both the strengths and weaknesses of the three papers, this section outlines what a more rigorous and defensible Catholic position on the canon should look like.

### A. What the Papers Should Have Argued (and Still Should)

1. **The Septuagint, not the later Masoretic Text, was the Bible of the Apostles.** The Dead Sea Scrolls demonstrate textual pluralism in the first century. The Hebrew canon was not fixed. The Septuagint's inclusion of the Deuterocanonical material makes it plausible—even likely—that the Apostles and the early Church regarded these books as part of their Scripture.

2. **The early Church used the Deuterocanonical books extensively and theologically.** Even if the allusions in Hebrews and James are not definitive proof of canonicity, the patristic record—especially Augustine's use of Wisdom for original sin and 2 Maccabees for prayers for the dead—is undeniable. The Fathers treated these books as Scripture.

3. **The Jamnia argument is a dead letter.** No serious scholar defends a formal "Council of Jamnia" anymore. Any argument resting on it should be abandoned.

4. **Trent did not invent the canon.** It dogmatized a tradition that stretched back to at least the late fourth century in the West. Whether one accepts Trent's authority is a separate question; the historical fact of the pre-Tridentine tradition is not in dispute.

5. **The Protestant canon has a problem.** The 39-book Old Testament canon rests on a Hebrew canon that was not definitively closed until after the New Testament was written, and which the early Church did not follow. Protestant apologists need a better account of how they arrived at their 39 books than simply appealing to "the Hebrew canon" as if it were a fixed, first-century reality.

### B. What the Papers Should Stop Arguing

1. **Athanasius did not hold a two-tiered canon—he held *no* Deuterocanon in his canon.** Acknowledge this honestly, then argue that Athanasius does not represent the consensus.

2. **Jerome was not a marginal voice.** Engage his textual arguments seriously. The *Hebraica veritas* is a legitimate scholarly concern.

3. **Hippo and Carthage did not "settle" the canon.** They reflected Western practice. Trent was the first dogmatic definition.

4. **Allusions are not citations.** The New Testament's use of Deuterocanonical material shows influence, not necessarily canonical recognition.

5. **Mockery is not argument.** Ad hominem rhetoric has no place in scholarly theology.

---

## IV. Conclusion: Toward a More Honest Debate

The three papers analyzed here contain significant historical and theological truths: the Jamnia myth should be abandoned; the Septuagint was the Bible of the early Church; the Deuterocanonical books were used theologically by the Fathers; and Trent did not invent the canon. These are genuine contributions to the debate.

However, the papers also contain significant errors: they misrepresent Athanasius's position, diminish Jerome's scholarly contributions, overstate the probative force of New Testament allusions, falsely claim that Hippo and Carthage "settled" the canon, and occasionally resort to rhetorical excess that undermines their credibility.

A more honest defense of the Catholic canon would:

- Acknowledge the genuine ambiguities in the patristic period.
- Engage Jerome's textual arguments on their merits.
- Recognize that the canon developed gradually, not through a single conciliar act.
- Focus on the *use* and *reception* of the Deuterocanonical books rather than claiming they were universally recognized as canonical from the start.
- Avoid ad hominem rhetoric and treat opponents with the intellectual respect they deserve.

The Catholic position on the canon is historically defensible. It does not need to be exaggerated, and it certainly does not need to be defended by misrepresenting the evidence. The truth is strong enough.
